Flash Fiction

Cereal Walker

By Alec McGlone

His fist slammed down hard, shaking the table and the plates set upon it. It landed square in the center of a black half circle sitting apart from the brown wood of the table, as though he had aimed for it, like a target. The remnants of a burning pot. The outburst cut off his parents and made his younger brother sitting across from him jolt backwards in surprise.

“I’m going, whether you like it or not.” He said.

“To hell you are if you keep this up. Go to your room and show respect. You are grounded for the next two weeks. Got it?” His mother said.

“I hate this! Why won’t you let me go? They’re going to make fun of me for not being there.” He exclaimed.

“Hate it all you want but this is our decision, period.” She said putting emphasis on period.

It cut through him like a razor. He stood up from the table and took his plate with him. He left it in the kitchen and muttering obscenities, went toward his bedroom. The small house creaked with every step he took. He had lived there so long he’s learned where to avoid stepping to make less noise when walking through it. He was not noiseless in his gait now, but nearly stomping his way through. He went up the stairs and found his room. The lock on the outside of his door reminded him of the situation he was in. His parents had been ones to lock him in his room for hours on end when he was acting up and he feared they would do it again. All because he wanted to go to a party his friend was having this weekend. It would be his first high school party and he was desperate to go. Instead he was told no and grounded. He ought to kick down the door if they do lock it, he thought.

The evening passed in silence, the echoes of footsteps cutting through the house, keeping it aware of the movements of those within its walls. Beds were found and the family laid to rest, until a boy stirred in the middle of the night.

The eldest boy, who had had the argument did not wake up, but his body moved without his consciousness. His eyes opened in his bed but did not focus. He sat up straight and mumbled something incoherent.

The last time he sleepwalked he had nearly fallen down the stairs. The stumble caused a ruckus and woke the whole house. The parents and younger brother found him in the kitchen, eyes blank but open, making himself a bowl of cereal. His brother called him the Cereal Walker the next day and laughed at him.

The boy left his bed and went to his door. He pulled it open with no struggle and moved through the house like a shadow in the night. He succeeded in making his way down the stairs without tripping. At the bottom, guided by the moonlight striking its way through the curtains into the house, found the kitchen. He opened a drawer and pulled an object from it. The glint of moonlight beamed from the metal and traveled across the room as he moved it.

With his new found possession at his side the boy maneuvered his way back to the stairs. The blank unfocused eyes guided the sleeping body up and up, towards the next floor and the door that held his anger.

Without a knock, his left hand turned the knob and opened the pathway to a room with no moonlight bleeding through any curtains. The darkness was absolute the deeper the boy went into the room. But guided by instinct alone he made his way to the bedside. The object in his right hand angered the house as he raised it, for with his weight shifting on his legs, the house let out a noisy creek underfoot.

A pair of white eyes opened slowly from the resting body of the mother, sleeping on her side, facing the edge of the bed. Within the darkness the object moved. The boy slammed his closed fist down on the bed, aiming for the center of a blackened mark. But the mark was not black, it was only shrouded in darkness. His aim however, was true, and the object hit its mark, the center of his mother’s turned body, underneath her arms and on the side of her stomach.

There was no glint from this metal, no beam of moonlight to fracture the darkness of the room. There was only a grunt as metal pierced skin and sank deep into the body of the one who had told him no.

His mother flailed her limbs in reaction to the knife cutting deep. The spasm woke her husband, lying next to her.

“Kevin?” The voice echoed weakly in the quiet dark house.

The sleepwalker pulled the knife out, and aimed again for the body lying in front of him.

The father yelped seeing his wife in pain. He looked to the sleepwalker, knife raised, ready to strike, and reached out, punching him in the face.

The knife clattered to the floor and the unfocused eyes cut through the darkness, focusing once more.

They found his mother’s eyes. Her confused whites searched the room for what had happened to her, only to land on the gaze of a boy who had just woken from a most pleasant dream.

“Cereal Walker,” He said.
